# Queensland Recreational Boating Knowledge Base (RMDL and PWCL)

This knowledge base paraphrases the official Queensland BoatSafe study materials for the Recreational Marine Driver Licence (RMDL) and Personal Watercraft Licence (PWCL). It is organised by teaching topic. Every fact is restated in original wording; no source prose is reproduced verbatim.

Conventions used in this file:

- Facts marked **[likely tested]** are the kind of specific, numeric or counter-intuitive facts that licence assessments and quiz questions target.
- Source citations appear at the end of each cluster of facts in the form (Workbook s4.3, Rules of the road). "Workbook" means the BoatSafe Workbook 6th edition. Other sources: RMDL Standard, PWCL Standard, Safety Equipment Factsheet, Pre-Departure Factsheet, DRSABCD Factsheet, QFES Fire Sheet, Water Limits Maps, Glossary, Voyage Plan Template.
- Key legislation: Transport Operations (Marine Safety) Act 1994 (TOMSA) and Transport Operations (Marine Safety) Regulation 2016 give effect to the international collision regulations (COLREGS) in Queensland and define water limits (Schedules 7, 8 and 9). Pollution is governed by the Transport Operations (Marine Pollution) Act 1995 and Regulation 2008.

## 1. Licences and who needs them

### RMDL

- A recreational marine driver licence (RMDL) is needed to operate a registrable recreational boat powered by an engine of more than 4.5 kW. **[likely tested]**
- Note the two different thresholds: registration is triggered at 3 kW or more, but the licence is only required above 4.5 kW. A boat with a 4 kW motor must be registered but can be driven unlicensed. **[likely tested]**
- Minimum age for an RMDL is 16 years. Younger people may complete a BoatSafe course but cannot be issued the qualification until they turn 16. **[likely tested]**
- The licence is obtained by completing a competency based BoatSafe course run by a BoatSafe Training Organisation (BTO) accredited by the Department of Transport and Main Roads. All candidates are assessed against the same core competencies. Recognition of prior learning (RPL) can exempt applicants from some or all training.
- After passing, the candidate receives a statement of competency. This is not the licence. It must be presented at a Transport and Main Roads customer service centre within 6 months of completing the course, or the person must be reassessed by a BTO. **[likely tested]**
- Queensland marine licences are virtual: there is no requirement to carry physical evidence of the licence. Queensland driver licences issued since July 2006 carry a marine licence indicator, and a verification certificate can be requested online for a small fee.
(Workbook Introduction, Licensing requirements)

### PWCL

- Every personal watercraft (PWC, for example a jet ski) operator must hold a separate personal watercraft licence (PWCL). Compared with other registered boat types, PWC are involved in the highest percentage of marine incidents, which is why the extra licence exists. **[likely tested]**
- Prerequisite for PWCL training and issue: hold an RMDL (or recognised equivalent such as a Speed Boat Driver Licence, a commercial marine licence, or an equivalent interstate licence), or be assessed competent in the RMDL BoatSafe units. There is no minimum time you must have held the RMDL first. **[likely tested]**
- A person may operate a PWC without a licence only if accompanied by a licensed operator who can take immediate control, or in limited cases on a PWC hired from a licensed hirer under the hirer's stated restrictions.
- A learner PWC driver may not carry any passenger other than the supervising PWC licence holder, and the licence holder must wear the kill switch lanyard while the learner drives. **[likely tested]**
- People moving to Queensland from interstate need a Queensland PWCL unless they hold an equivalent interstate PWC licence. Short term visitors may use their home state equivalent if that is all their home state requires.
(Workbook Introduction and s7.1, Licensing; PWCL Standard, Introduction)

### Licences for towing

- The operator of any boat towing a waterskier or other towed person must hold a valid marine licence. (Workbook s4.10, Waterskiing rules)

## 2. Registration

### Which boats must be registered

- All recreational boats fitted with a motor or auxiliary engine of 3 kW or more must be registered to be operated on Queensland waters. Boats with engines under 3 kW do not require registration. **[likely tested]**
- Registration is lodged with Transport and Main Roads customer service centres. Fees are calculated by boat length. Exemptions exist for totally and permanently incapacitated war pensioners, and concessions apply for some pensioners and Seniors Card holders (one boat).
- Boats 15 metres and over need Maritime Safety Queensland endorsement before registration, transfer or change of particulars can be processed.
- When a registered boat is sold, the new owner must lodge a transfer of registration application within 14 days. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s1.9, Registration)

### Registration symbols and labels

- Registration symbols must be clearly visible, in plain characters, in a colour contrasting with the hull.
- Boats capable of planing (speed boats, dinghies): symbols at least 150 mm high on both sides. **[likely tested]**
- All other boats excluding PWC: symbols at least 75 mm high on both sides or on the stern. **[likely tested]**
- The registration label goes on the exterior of the boat, on the stern or port side, near the symbols.
- PWC: symbols on both sides, at least 100 mm high, legible from 30 m, in a contrasting colour, easily seen while under way. The PWC label goes on the port side near the symbols. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s1.9, Registration; Workbook s7.2, PWC Registration)

### Tenders

- A tender to a registered Queensland ship is exempt from registration provided it is used within 2 nautical miles of the primary boat. **[likely tested]**
- The tender must be marked with the word "tender" plus the primary boat's registration symbols at least 75 mm high on the exterior (or inside in the largest practicable characters if the exterior is not possible). It may carry the owner's name instead if used with more than one of the owner's boats.
- A tender that does not require registration only needs to carry one item of safety equipment: a light for signalling. Equipping it further is recommended. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s1.9, Registration; Safety Equipment Factsheet)

### Australian Builders Plate (ABP) and flotation

- All new recreational craft manufactured in or imported into Queensland since September 2006 must display an Australian Builders Plate (with some exceptions such as sailing ships). **[likely tested]**
- The ABP shows: builder name and Hull Identification Number or build date; maximum outboard engine power (kW or hp); maximum outboard engine weight (kg); maximum number of persons (as a whole number and in kilograms); maximum load (kg); for boats under 6 m a buoyancy statement; and a warning that alterations may invalidate the plate.
- The plate must be permanently fixed and readily visible to the operator near the steering position. A penalty can apply if it is missing, unreadable or wrongly located.
- All boats manufactured after 1 July 2006 must have some form of flotation, either basic or level. After 1 July 2006 the term "inadequate flotation" is no longer permitted on plates. **[likely tested]**
- Basic flotation: the swamped or capsized boat floats in some form, so occupants can cling to the hull. Level flotation: the swamped boat floats level and resists capsize in calm water, so occupants can stay aboard and bail. Boats with no inbuilt buoyancy can sink within seconds.
(Workbook s1.7, Buoyancy; s1.8 to s1.10)

### Capacity

- The ABP person capacity assumes good conditions and smooth waters. In open waters or rough conditions, reduce the number of people carried by about one third. **[likely tested]**
- Overloading lowers freeboard (the vertical distance from the water surface to the gunwale) and is one of the easiest ways to swamp or capsize a boat. Heavy items should be stowed low and central, weight distributed evenly, and extra fuel and water weight accounted for.
(Workbook s1.10, Capacity specification)

## 3. General safety obligations

### The general safety obligation (GSO)

- The GSO is the core maritime safety principle: the owner and operator must ensure the boat is safe, properly equipped and crewed, and operated in a safe manner. Breaching it can lead to prosecution and heavy penalties. **[likely tested]**
- Examples of breaches: overloading a boat that then swamps; failing to show passengers where lifejackets are kept and how to don them; being unable to navigate (read a chart, use a compass) and getting lost.
- Seaworthiness is part of the GSO and includes: suitability of the boat and propulsion for the activity and area; physical condition of the hull; proper loading with adequate freeboard; carrying required safety equipment; operating within the limits of skipper and crew; and complying with regulations including correct navigation lights at night.
(Workbook s1.8, Seaworthiness; s1.11, General safety obligation)

### Speed and distance rules (boats other than PWC)

These apply statewide whether or not signs are present. You must not exceed 6 knots:

- within 30 m of boats that are anchored, moored, made fast to the shore, or aground **[likely tested]**
- within 30 m of a jetty, wharf, pontoon or boat ramp **[likely tested]**
- within 30 m of people in the water **[likely tested]**
- in harbours and marinas. **[likely tested]**

Related rules:

- Boats must stay at least 30 m from a diver in the water when a Code A (Alpha) flag is shown on a float or structure. **[likely tested]**
- Slow to 6 knots or less within 100 m of a cross-river cable ferry, sound one long blast before passing astern, and preferably wait until the ferry reaches the shore. In Queensland the ferry generally shows a flashing red light while its cables obstruct passage. **[likely tested]**
- Judging distance: 30 m is about ten boat lengths for a 3 m boat, or five lengths for a 6 m boat.
- Wash: you are responsible for the effect of your wash on other boats, people in the water and the shore. Complying with a posted speed limit does not guarantee your wash is acceptable. **[likely tested]**
- Local rules on speed, anchoring, sewage and PWC use may also apply; check signage, local maritime authorities, and the Beacon to Beacon Directory.
(Workbook s4.7, Local rules; s4.5, Navigation lights, ferries)

### Alcohol and drugs

- The blood alcohol limit on the water is the same as on the road: under 0.05. **[likely tested]**
- Queensland Water Police may breath test operators on the water, at boat ramps and in marinas.
- The master is responsible for the safe operation of the boat even when someone else is driving, so the master must stay under 0.05 at all times and ensure any person driving is also under the limit. A master can be convicted while a sober person drives. **[likely tested]**
- "Drug" includes prescription medications. Seasickness, hayfever and allergy medicines can cause drowsiness, especially mixed with alcohol.
- Alcohol effects are amplified on the water by sun, wind, waves and motion. Penalties range from fines to imprisonment, and the marine licence can be suspended or cancelled.
(Workbook s4.11, Alcohol and drug obligations)

### Marine incidents and reporting

- A marine incident must be reported to a shipping inspector within 48 hours of the incident, on the approved form (marine incident report form F3071), unless there is a reasonable excuse. **[likely tested]**
- Shipping inspectors include Maritime Safety Queensland Marine Officers, Queensland Water Police, and Queensland Boating and Fisheries Patrol officers.
- Reportable incidents (defined under TOMSA s123) include: loss of a person from a boat; death or grievous bodily harm caused by a boat's operations; loss, presumed loss or abandonment of a boat; collision; stranding; material damage to or by a boat; danger to a person; danger of serious damage to a boat or structure. **[likely tested]**
- Incidents involving injury to people must always be reported even if the boat is undamaged.
- For loss, presumed loss or abandonment of a ship, the owner reports; for all other incident types, the master reports. **[likely tested]**
- Failing to report is an offence: maximum penalty 40 penalty units for an individual and 200 for a company (one penalty unit was $121.90 as at 19 January 2017).
- When an incident involves two or more boats, the master must (TOMSA s124): render assistance where possible without endangering their own passengers, stay at the scene, give the owner's details to the other boat's operator, assist injured people, and assist the owner of any damaged property. **[likely tested]**
- All boaters have a legal obligation to assist persons in distress unless they are unable, assistance is not required, or circumstances make it unreasonable or unsafe for their own boat.
- Insurers commonly refuse claims if the incident was not officially reported.
(Workbook s4.13, Marine incident reporting; RMDL Standard Unit 4)

### Pollution

- Maritime Safety Queensland manages ship-sourced pollution out to 3 nautical miles from the territorial sea baseline under the Transport Operations (Marine Pollution) Act 1995; beyond that, Commonwealth law applies. Penalties can reach 5000 penalty units for an individual and 100 000 for a company. **[likely tested]**
- Garbage: nothing is to be discharged overboard; stow it and dispose of it ashore. Plastics (ropes, lines, nets, bags) must never go overboard. Fresh fish and fish parts, and small food scraps used deliberately for fish feeding, are not counted as garbage.
- Oil and chemicals must not be discharged. Watch bilge pump discharges, use absorbent pads for oily bilge water, leave about 2 percent space in fuel tanks for expansion, and never hose spilt fuel into the water.
- Sewage highlights: no discharge of any sewage (treated or untreated) in boat harbours, canals, marinas, and designated marine national park zones. Untreated sewage: nil discharge in smooth waters for all ships; in open waters nil within 1 nm of aquaculture resources, and for ships with 7 to 15 people aboard nil within 1 nm of a reef, an island or mainland low water mark; ships with 16 or more people cannot discharge untreated sewage in open waters at all. Where discharge is permitted, sewage from a fixed toilet must first pass through a macerator. Treated sewage: grade C nil within 0.5 nm (926 m) of a person in the water, aquaculture resources or a reef; grade B nil within 700 m of the same; grade A unrestricted outside prohibited waters. **[likely tested]**
- All marine pollution incidents must be reported to the local Regional Harbour Master's office (when, where, substance, extent, boat details).
(Workbook s4.14, Pollution)

### Noise, wildlife and wrecks

- Maximum engine noise for recreational ships including PWC is 85 dB(A) at 30 m. Noise complaints go to the environment department, not MSQ. **[likely tested]**
- Whales and dolphins: do not approach closer than 100 m. Within 300 m: hold a constant speed of no more than 5 knots, avoid sudden course changes, do not approach head on, do not sit in the animal's path, do not separate a group or a mother and calf. **[likely tested]**
- Go slow (displacement speed) and keep a lookout in turtle and dugong protection areas; do not drive across shallow seagrass beds.
- Any ship that sank more than 75 years ago is a protected historic wreck. Entering a declared protected zone, damaging, disturbing, anchoring on, or removing objects from a wreck are offences. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s4.14 and s4.15)

## 4. Safety equipment

### Framework

- Required equipment depends on boat type (not requiring registration, registered, PWC) and water type (smooth, partially smooth, beyond/open). "Required" means it must be carried; "recommended" means it is suggested in order to meet the general safety obligation. Equip for the entire trip including waters you only pass through. **[likely tested]**
- All equipment must be in good working order; check service and expiry dates on EPIRBs, fire extinguishers, flares and inflatable lifejackets.
- The master must tell every person on board where safety equipment is kept.
(Workbook s2.6, Safety equipment table; Safety Equipment Factsheet)

### Lifejackets

Types and standards:

- Level 275, 150 and 100 jackets have a flotation collar to keep the head above water, high visibility colours and reflective tape; usable in all waters, best offshore. Level 50 has the same general purpose but no collar, in visible colours; suited to waterskiing, PWC, sailing dinghies, where the wearer is in the water briefly; it will not keep an unconscious person face up. Level 50 special purpose has level 50 buoyancy but less visible colours (may be a buoyancy wetsuit); not recommended for general boating because it is harder to spot in a search. Coastal and SOLAS jackets keep the body afloat for long periods, with reflective tape and a whistle; carried mostly by commercial and long distance boats. **[likely tested]**
- The current standard is AS 4758, which replaced AS 1512-1996, AS 1499-1996 and AS 2260-1996. Old-standard jackets remain acceptable while in good condition. Mapping: old PFD type 1 equals level 275, 150 or 100; PFD type 2 equals level 50; PFD type 3 equals level 50 special purpose; coastal lifejacket equals level 275 or 150. **[likely tested]**
- Required markings include the level, manufacturer identification, body mass range, donning instructions, and gas cylinder information for inflatables. Inflatable jackets need periodic gas cylinder servicing. A pouch style "bum bag" inflatable must be unpacked and worn around the neck ready for inflation whenever wearing is compulsory (for example a bar crossing).

Carriage and wearing rules:

- One lifejacket of appropriate size must be carried for each person aged 12 months and over, except a diver wearing an inflatable diver jacket on a boat engaged in diving. **[likely tested]**
- Children under 12 must wear a lifejacket when underway (including drifting) in an open boat under 4.8 m. **[likely tested]**
- Everyone (aged one year and over) must wear a lifejacket while crossing a designated coastal bar in an open boat under 4.8 m. **[likely tested]**
- Lifejacket levels by water and craft: smooth waters, registered boat - level 275, 150, 100, 50 or 50 special purpose required (recommended only for boats not requiring registration); PWC - level 50 or level 50 special purpose. Partially smooth waters, registered boat - level 275, 150, 100 or 50 required; PWC - level 50. Beyond partially smooth waters, registered boat - level 275, 150 or 100 required (level 50 no longer acceptable); PWC - level 50. **[likely tested]**
- PWC operators and passengers must wear their lifejacket at all times. **[likely tested]**
- Skiers and towed people: at least level 50, level 50 special purpose, or an approved buoyancy wetsuit in smooth waters; at least level 50 in partially smooth waters (a level 100 is also acceptable). **[likely tested]**
- Exemptions: lifejackets are not required on a ship (other than a PWC) that has a level flotation statement in the approved form, operating in a river, creek, stream or within breakwaters or revetments, and fitted with grab lines or rails giving each person a secure hold. A registered tender with a level flotation statement needs no lifejackets within 1 km of the parent ship in smooth or partially smooth waters, or within 500 m of the parent ship beyond partially smooth waters. **[likely tested]**
- Storage: lifejackets must be clearly visible to passengers or readily accessible and marked by a sign with the word "lifejackets" in red letters on a white background or white on red. **[likely tested]**
- Wearing is also wise (not compulsory) at the first sign of bad weather, in emergencies, between sunset and sunrise, in unfamiliar waters, with a following sea, when boating alone, and always on poor swimmers and children.
(Workbook s2.8, Safety equipment - lifejackets; Safety Equipment Factsheet)

### EPIRBs

- An EPIRB (emergency position indicating radio beacon) of the 406 MHz digital type is required on all boats, including PWC and boats not requiring registration, when operating beyond smooth and partially smooth waters and more than 2 nautical miles from land. **[likely tested]**
- The old analogue 121.5 MHz beacons are no longer monitored by satellite. About 98 percent of 121.5 MHz alerts detected in Australia were false alarms; 406 MHz beacons carry a digital identity that lets AusSAR phone the owner to check. **[likely tested]**
- EPIRBs must comply with AS/NZS 4280.1:2003, must show a printed expiry date and be serviced or replaced by the manufacturer or agent by that date, and must be registered with the Australian Maritime Safety Authority (AMSA) in the name of the owner or master. AMSA registration must be renewed every 2 years. Proof of registration must be shown to marine inspectors. **[likely tested]**
- Performance: transmits continuously for at least 48 hours once activated; geostationary satellites can alert search authorities within about 3 minutes, orbiting satellites within about 90 minutes on average; position accuracy is roughly 5 km, improving to about 120 m with built-in GPS. **[likely tested]**
- Activation: use only as a last resort after radio, flares, V sheet and phone. For manual activation the antenna must be vertical with a clear sky view; a red flashing light after about 3 minutes shows it is transmitting. Water-activated models can also be triggered manually before the boat sinks.
- Accidental activation: switch it off and call the Rescue Coordination Centre on 1800 641 792, or advise police or the local volunteer marine rescue group. Rescue authorities respond to about 7000 activations a year and every one triggers a response.
- Personal (pocket) EPIRBs are not a lawful substitute for the required EPIRB. Approved EPIRBs float upright with the aerial pointing skyward; a personal EPIRB tips on its side in the water unless held, sharply reducing detection.
- Disposal: remove the battery and disable the beacon; old beacons can be dropped at Battery World stores. Beacons in landfill can self-activate and divert rescue resources.
(Workbook s2.8, Signalling equipment - EPIRBs; Safety Equipment Factsheet)

### Flares, V sheet and other signalling gear

- Required flare kit (registered boats and PWC in partially smooth waters and beyond): two red hand flares and two hand held orange smoke signals. **[likely tested]**
- A red hand flare can be seen up to about 6 nm (10 km) away; an orange smoke signal is a daytime-only signal visible up to about 2 nm (4 km) on a clear day. **[likely tested]**
- Only in-date flares count toward the required equipment. Expired flares can be handed in at listed disposal locations. Severe penalties apply for misuse of flares, and offenders can be billed for the resulting search costs. Store flares in a waterproof container away from heat, hold them downwind when lit, and cool spent flares in the sea.
- V sheet: a fluorescent orange-red sheet with a large black V, spread on deck, flown as a flag, or tied over an upturned hull to signal distress to aircraft and boats. Required for registered boats and PWC in partially smooth and open waters. **[likely tested]**
- PWC exception: the V sheet and flares are not required for a PWC operating in an approved aquatic event, or beyond partially smooth waters within 0.5 nm of land. **[likely tested]**
- Signalling device (a torch, fluorescent light, lantern or cyalume stick) is required on every boat, in every water type, when operating between sunset and sunrise. It is not a substitute for navigation lights. **[likely tested]**
- Dye markers create a yellow stain visible by day only, dispersing after about 20 minutes.
(Workbook s2.8; Safety Equipment Factsheet)

### Fire fighting equipment

- Fire fighting equipment capable of extinguishing a fire quickly and effectively is required on registered boats in all waters, and specifically on all boats over 5 m. Every boat carrying fuel should have at least one dry powder extinguisher mounted in an accessible dry position, away from likely fire sources. **[likely tested]**
- Check the fill gauge and shake dry powder extinguishers regularly so the powder does not compact. Small extinguishers can be bagged in plastic against salt spray. A fire blanket and a sturdy bucket with about 2 m of lanyard are valuable additions, and a fire blanket should be carried where a cooking device is installed, stored away from the stove.
(Workbook s2.8, Fire fighting equipment; Pre-Departure Factsheet)

Fire extinguisher types (band colours under AS/NZS 1841.1:2007):

- Water: all red body; class A only.
- Foam: blue band; classes B and A only.
- Dry chemical powder: white band; classes B and E, and AB(E) powder also covers class A. This is the general purpose choice. A 1 kg dry chemical extinguisher discharges for only about 10 to 12 seconds. **[likely tested]**
- Carbon dioxide: black band; classes E and B; noisy, cold, high pressure discharge.
- Vaporising liquid: yellow band; classes E, A and B (old BCF types withdrawn).
- Wet chemical: oatmeal band; class F (cooking oils and fats) and A.

Classes of fire: A carbonaceous solids (wood, paper, cloth, plastics); B flammable and combustible liquids (petrol, oil, paint); C combustible gases (LPG, butane, propane); D combustible metals; E electrical (not an official fuel class but a hazard overlay); F cooking oils and fats. **[likely tested]**

Using an extinguisher - the PASS method: Pull the pin, Aim the nozzle at the base of the fire, Squeeze the handle, Sweep side to side at the base. Only fight a fire if the agent suits the fuel, electricity has been considered, you can put it out quickly, your escape is not at risk, and everyone else has been evacuated. Extinguishers should be pressure tested professionally every 5 years. **[likely tested]**
(QFES Fire Sheet)

### Anchors, bailing, oars, water, navigation gear

Anchor scale (recommended, with cable meaning chain, rope or both; boats over 5 m need at least 2 m of chain between anchor and rope):

- Smooth waters: under 5 m - one anchor with at least 18 m of cable; 5 m to under 8 m - one anchor with at least 27 m; 8 m and over - two anchors with at least 37 m each. **[likely tested]**
- Partially smooth and beyond: under 5 m - one anchor with at least 27 m (note the step up from 18 m); other sizes as above. **[likely tested]**

Bailing and pumping (recommended in all waters): under 5 m - suitable bailing equipment; 5 m to under 8 m - a bilge pump of 45 L per minute capacity; 8 m and over - a bilge pump of 70 L per minute. **[likely tested]**

- Manual propulsion (oars or paddles) is recommended for boats under 6 m. **[likely tested]**
- Drinking water: carry enough for everyone on board for the trip, plus extra for emergencies (recommended in all waters).
- Navigation equipment (recommended in partially smooth and open waters): a chart and a liquid damped compass appropriate to the area, or other direction finding or positioning equipment. A PWC without a chart or compass should carry an electronic navigation device. **[likely tested]**
- At least one solidly built metal or plastic bucket with 2 m of rope should be carried on any ship; it doubles as bailer and fire bucket. Bilge pumps are recommended for boats 5 m and over.
- Larger boats going to sea: consider a liferaft (stowed in a quick release housing), lifebuoys with lights and buoyant lines, a rescue quoit on a line, safety harnesses for bluewater sailing, a first aid kit in a waterproof box, an air horn or other efficient sound signal, and a tool kit.
(Workbook s2.6 and s2.8; Safety Equipment Factsheet)

## 5. Water limits

### Definitions

- Smooth waters: rivers, creeks, streams and lakes; waters within breakwaters or revetments; waters within 0.5 nautical mile from land inside partially smooth limits; plus other waters listed in Schedule 8 of the Regulation. **[likely tested]**
- Partially smooth waters: waters determined by Maritime Safety Queensland and described in Schedule 7, excluding the waters within 0.5 nm of land (which count as smooth). **[likely tested]**
- Open waters: everything beyond smooth and partially smooth limits.
- The EPIRB line: an EPIRB must be carried beyond smooth and partially smooth waters when more than 2 nm from land. The 2 nm belt applies around any land above the highest water mark, including islands too small to show on the maps. **[likely tested]**
- Source of truth: Transport Operations (Marine Safety) Act 1994 and Regulation 2016, Schedule 7 (partially smooth), Schedule 8 (smooth), Schedule 9 (dictionary) and Part 2 (EPIRB).
(Workbook s2.7, Water limits; Water Limits Maps, cover)

- Cooktown

- Cairns (Trinity Bay and Trinity Inlet, out toward Green and Fitzroy Islands)
- Mourilyan (Mourilyan Harbour)
- Palm Islands - Lucinda - Rockingham Bay (Hinchinbrook Channel, Cardwell, Halifax Bay)
- Townsville (Cleveland Bay, Magnetic Island)
- Upstart Bay (Burdekin)
- Bowen - Whitsunday - Repulse Bay (Airlie Beach, Whitsunday Passage, Edgecumbe Bay)
- Sand Bay and St Helens Bay (Seaforth, Cape Hillsborough)
- Llewellyn Bay - Mackay - Hay Point (Pioneer River, Sarina coast)

Practical reading of the maps: enclosed bays such as Moreton Bay, Hervey Bay, Cleveland Bay and the Whitsunday passage waters are partially smooth; rivers, creeks and the 0.5 nm coastal strip within them are smooth; the red EPIRB line runs 2 nm seaward of land beyond the partially smooth limits. **[likely tested]**
(Water Limits Maps, all sheets; Workbook s2.7)

## 6. Give-way rules (collision regulations)

### Framework

- The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGS) are the traffic laws of the sea and apply equally to every boat afloat. Queensland gives them effect through TOMSA 1994 and the 2016 Regulation, adding local rules mainly on speed and wash. Significant penalties apply for breaches.
- Never assume the other skipper will follow the rules; do whatever is necessary to avoid a collision, with actions clear and deliberate. **[likely tested]**
- Keep a proper lookout by sight and sound at all times. Travel at a safe speed for visibility, traffic, hazards, depth, manoeuvrability and background lighting; the faster you go, the greater the safe distance you need.
- Risk of collision test: if the compass bearing of an approaching boat stays virtually steady, a collision risk exists; take early, positive action (change course and/or speed). **[likely tested]**

### Power boat encounters

- Head on: both boats alter course to starboard (right), never to port. Make the turn big enough to be obvious. **[likely tested]**
- Crossing: the boat on your right (starboard side) is the stand-on vessel. As give-way vessel, keep clear by altering course or slowing to pass astern of the other boat. **[likely tested]**
- Stand-on vessel duties: hold course and speed so you are predictable; but if the give-way boat does not act, you must take action to avoid collision. This does not relieve the other boat of its obligation. **[likely tested]**
- Overtaking: the overtaking boat may pass on either side but must keep well clear and must not cut in front after passing. This applies whether the overtaking boat is power or sail: a sailing boat overtaking a power boat gives way. **[likely tested]**

### Sailing boat rules

- Wind on different sides: the boat with the wind on its port side gives way.
- Wind on the same side: the windward boat keeps clear of the leeward boat. The windward side is the side opposite the mainsail.
- If a port tack boat cannot tell which tack a windward boat is on, it keeps clear. **[likely tested]**

### Power gives way to

- Sailing boats (unless the sailing boat is overtaking, or the powered vessel is large with limited manoeuvrability)
- Boats engaged in commercial fishing or trawling
- Large ships constrained by their draught in channels
- Vessels restricted in their ability to manoeuvre because of their work
- Vessels not under command. **[likely tested]**
These vessels are recognised by day shapes by day and special lights at night. A simple rule for small power boats: keep clear of any vessel showing extra lights.

### Channels and shipping

- In narrow channels keep to the starboard (right hand) side and pass oncoming boats port to port. There is no need to swerve right if boats are already passing at a wide distance; the rule removes doubt in close situations. **[likely tested]**
- Do not anchor in channels, especially near markers. Small boats keep clear of big ships that cannot leave the channel.
- If you must travel along a shipping channel, keep to its outer edge and monitor VHF channels 12 and 16 for traffic information. Cross a channel at right angles, behind a large ship, when clear. **[likely tested]**
- Big ship realities: about 15 minutes can elapse between a ship appearing on the horizon and potential impact; a large ship can need up to 2 nautical miles (about 37 football fields) to stop; a ship with its bridge aft has a bow blind spot several hundred metres long. The Port of Brisbane handles about 7000 ship movements a year. **[likely tested]**
- Diver down (code flag A displayed): keep at least 30 m clear and slow down. Night diving boats show the lights of a vessel restricted in ability to manoeuvre (red, white, red in a vertical line). **[likely tested]**
- Restricted visibility: slow down or stop, use common sense, be ready to act immediately.
(Workbook s4.3, Rules of the road; s4.9, Navigating near shipping channels)

## 7. Buoyage and marks (IALA A)

The IALA Buoyage System A is the water traffic sign system used in Queensland (and Australia). Each mark type has a distinctive combination of colour, shape, topmark and light. Study them on the chart as well as on the water. A key counter-intuitive point taught in the course: compared to road driving, the buoyage system can feel like driving on the wrong side of the road. **[likely tested]**

### Lateral marks

- Lateral marks show the port and starboard sides of a navigable channel. When a red and green mark sit close together as a pair, pass between them.
- Direction convention: travelling upstream (away from the sea), keep red port marks on your left and green starboard marks on your right. Travelling downstream (toward the sea), keep red marks on your right and green on your left. **[likely tested]**
- At night, port marks show a red light and starboard marks a green light; any flash rhythm may be used. **[likely tested]**
- Where the direction of buoyage is unclear (for example, a channel approachable from two seaward entrances), charts show a direction of buoyage arrow symbol. In the Gold Coast Broadwater, for instance, the Seaway is treated as the nearest sea entrance. **[likely tested]**

### Cardinal marks

- Cardinal marks show where the safest water lies relative to a danger, using compass directions: pass north of a north mark, east of an east mark, south of a south mark, west of a west mark. **[likely tested]**
- They are yellow and black with two black cone topmarks. Memory aid: the black band sits where the cones point. North: both cones point up (black on top). South: both point down (black on bottom). East: cones point outward, away from each other (black top and bottom). West: cones point inward, toward each other (black middle band). **[likely tested]**
- Lights are white, with a clock face mnemonic: east flashes 3, south flashes 6 followed by one long flash, west flashes 9, north flashes continuously. The long flash after south prevents confusion between south, east and west. **[likely tested]**

### Isolated danger marks

- Placed on or above an isolated danger (rock, reef, shoal, wreck) that has navigable water all around it. Do not pass close; check the chart for the extent of the danger.
- Topmark: two black spheres. Light: white, group of two flashes. Memory aid: two flashes, two spheres. **[likely tested]**

### Safe water marks

- Indicate navigable water all around, for example mid-channel of a large shipping fairway or a landfall buoy. In Queensland they mark the seaward start of fairways into major ports. **[likely tested]**
- Light: white, one long flash every 10 seconds. **[likely tested]**

### Special marks

- Yellow marks indicating a special area or feature: traffic separation, spoil grounds, cables or pipelines (including outfalls and groynes), a deep draught channel within a wider channel, or the intersection of two channels where lateral or cardinal marks would not suit. Consult the chart to know which side to pass. **[likely tested]**
- Light: yellow, with a rhythm different from any used by the white lights of cardinal, isolated danger and safe water marks.

### Leads, sector lights and signs

- Leads (leading marks or lights) are pairs of beacons which, when lined up, put you in the centre of a navigable channel, commonly guiding entry to ports and across difficult sections. Traditionally the front beacon is a triangle apex up and the rear beacon an inverted triangle; many modern leads instead show fixed white day lights, and blue lights are increasingly used at night where background lighting is heavy. **[likely tested]**
- Because leads mark the centre of the channel and the rules require keeping to starboard, open the leads slightly to starboard in larger channels, especially at night. **[likely tested]**
- Fixed blue lights also mark the centre of the navigable channel under bridges. **[likely tested]**
- Directional lights show through a narrow arc (less than about 6 degrees) like leads; sector lights show different coloured arcs warning of hazards and marking clear channels.
- Advisory signs cover anchoring prohibitions, speed limits and submarine cables. Anchoring is prohibited within 200 m of a submarine cable crossing; if your anchor snags near a cable sign, cut the line, do not haul it up. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s4.2, IALA buoyage system)

## 8. Navigation lights, day shapes and sound signals

### When lights are required

- Navigation lights must be shown from sunset to sunrise, whether under way or at anchor, and also during restricted visibility in daylight. **[likely tested]**
- "Under way" means not at anchor, not moored, not made fast to the shore, and not aground. A drifting boat is still under way and shows the same lights as when making way. **[likely tested]**
- Lights must be positioned so the superstructure does not obscure them; the masthead or all-round white light sits on the centreline where practical. Carry spare bulbs at night.

### Power boats under way

- Under 7 m and top speed 7 knots or less: an all-round white light, plus sidelights if possible. **[likely tested]**
- Under 12 m: either sidelights (separate or combined) with a masthead light and stern light, or sidelights with an all-round white light. **[likely tested]**
- 12 m to under 20 m: a masthead light, sidelights (separate or combined) and a stern light.
- Larger ships may carry two masthead lights, the higher one aft (over 50 m must).

### Sailing boats under way

- A sailing boat using its motor, even with sails up, is a power boat and shows power boat lights. **[likely tested]**
- Under 7 m: the same lights as larger sailing boats if practicable; otherwise a torch or lantern showing a white light kept ready to prevent collision.
- 7 m to 20 m: either a combined lantern at or near the masthead (sidelights and stern light in one), or separate sidelights and stern light.
- Over 20 m: separate sidelights and stern light (a combined lantern is not allowed), optionally with all-round red over green lights at the masthead. Any sailing boat with separate sidelights and stern light may add the red over green pair.

### Non-powered and anchored boats

- Rowed boats: a torch or lantern showing a white light, ready to display. Rowing craft training or racing on the Brisbane River between sunset and sunrise must carry an all-round white flashing light. **[likely tested]**
- At anchor under 50 m: one all-round white light where best seen. Over 50 m: two anchor lights, the higher one forward. Anchor lights are compulsory sunset to sunrise; show extra (non-navigation) lighting in busy anchorages. **[likely tested]**

### Lights and shapes that mean keep clear

- Extra lights above the basic set mean a vessel with special status: fishing or trawling, constrained by draught, restricted in ability to manoeuvre, not under command, or towing. A small boat's rule of thumb is to keep clear of anything showing additional lights. **[likely tested]**
- Towing: a tow over 200 m long changes the light and day shape configuration (diamond shape displayed); give towing operations a wide berth and never pass between tug and tow. **[likely tested]**
- Night diving: red, white, red all-round lights in a vertical line (restricted in ability to manoeuvre).

### Sound signals

Manoeuvring signals (mostly used by larger vessels, but all operators must recognise them):

- One short blast: I am altering course to starboard. **[likely tested]**
- Two short blasts: I am altering course to port. **[likely tested]**
- Three short blasts: my engines are going astern (stopping or reversing). **[likely tested]**
- Five or more short blasts: I am unsure of your intentions (danger or doubt). **[likely tested]**

Fog signals in restricted visibility, sounded at least every 2 minutes:

- One long blast: power boat under way and making way.
- Two long blasts: power boat under way but stopped (not making way).
- One long plus two short: sailing, fishing or working vessels making way. **[likely tested]**

To attract attention you may use any light or sound signal that cannot be mistaken for an authorised signal, a white hand flare, or a light beam directed at the danger; never shine a light into another skipper's eyes.
(Workbook s4.4, Sound signals; s4.5, Navigation lights)

### International code of signals (selected single letter meanings)

- A (Alpha): I have a diver down; keep well clear at slow speed. **[likely tested]**
- B (Bravo): carrying, loading or discharging dangerous goods.
- O (Oscar): man overboard. **[likely tested]**
- V (Victor): I require assistance. **[likely tested]**
- W (Whiskey): I require medical assistance.
- U (Uniform): you are running into danger.
- NC (November Charlie together): international distress signal. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s4.1, International code of signals)

## 9. Anchoring

### Anchor types

- Danforth: the usual choice for small craft; light with excellent holding in mud and sand; avoid reefs where the flukes can jam. **[likely tested]**
- CQR or plough: used by larger, heavier boats; good holding in sand and mud; not for reef.
- Reef or grapnel anchors: designed for coral and rock; the prongs straighten under heavy load so the anchor can be recovered, and they do less damage to coral. In coral areas it is better to anchor on sand; anchor on coral only in an emergency. **[likely tested]**
- Sea anchor or drogue: a device streamed in heavy weather to slow drift and hold the bow into wind and waves; valuable offshore. A drogue from the bow will keep a disabled boat pointed into the waves; without power a boat drifts beam-on to the sea, which risks capsize in big seas. **[likely tested]**

### Ground tackle

- The anchor rode is the line and/or chain; the whole system (anchor, rope, chain, shackles) is the ground tackle.
- Always include at least 2 to 3 m of chain between anchor and rope: the chain keeps the shank parallel to the seabed so the flukes bite, and prevents the line chafing on the bottom. **[likely tested]**
- Do not use floating rope such as polypropylene for anchoring: it hinders the anchor setting and gets cut by propellers. Nylon is the best line (strong, stretchy, abrasion resistant); silver rope is acceptable but weaker. **[likely tested]**
- Mark the rode at intervals so you know how much is out. Secure the bitter end to the boat.

### Scope

- Scope is the ratio of rode paid out to water depth. Use 5 to 1 in normal conditions and up to 8 to 1 in extreme conditions; a flatter pull holds better. Example: anchoring in 10 m of water in average weather needs about 50 m of line. **[likely tested]**

### Technique

- Choose shelter with swinging room, check depth and bottom type, and consider how you will lie relative to other boats.
- Motor slowly into the wind or tide past the chosen spot, wait until the boat starts drifting back, lower (never throw) the anchor, pay out the scope, then motor gently astern to dig it in.
- Confirm the anchor is holding: watch a transit between two fixed shore features, watch the cable angle, or feel the rope for the vibration of dragging. Re-check regularly.
- Always anchor by the bow, never the stern: a stern anchor pulls the low-freeboard stern down and waves or wash can flood the boat. **[likely tested]**
- In small boats handle the anchor from the cockpit rather than the foredeck. Wear gloves when hauling.
- Recovery: motor slowly up the line, and break the anchor out with a pull or short push ahead when directly above it. If it is fouled, do not drive over the top of it to wrench it free, the boat can overturn; cut it away if it cannot be freed. **[likely tested]**
- Do not anchor in channels, near navigation beacons, near cable crossing signs, or on historic wrecks. Show an anchor light at night. Moorings belong to someone; penalties apply for unauthorised use. **[likely tested]**
- Allow for tide range when anchoring for a long stay, and remember wind and sea changes affect holding power.
(Workbook s3.5, To anchor; s2.8, Anchor)

## 10. Weather, tides and coastal bars

### Weather sources and products

- The Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) issues routine coastal waters forecasts covering waters within 60 nm of the coast, updated several times daily. Coastal warnings are issued whenever strong winds, gales, storm force or stronger winds are expected, and are renewed every 6 hours. **[likely tested]**
- Forecast content: warnings; synopsis of highs, lows and fronts for the next 36 hours; expected wind, sea and swell, and visibility; station observations.
- Forecast interpretation margins: gusts can be up to 40 percent stronger than the average wind speed given, and some waves will be up to twice the average height quoted. Sea and swell forecasts ignore local effects like currents, tidal flows and landforms. **[likely tested]**
- Weather is available by marine radio (27 MHz and VHF channel 67 in many areas, HF broadcasts on 8176 kHz), broadcast radio and TV, the BOM website, and MSQ's phone Maritime Weather Service: 1300 360 426 (all Queensland), 1300 360 427 (marine warnings), 1300 360 428 (south east Queensland).

### Reading a weather map

- Isobars join points of equal pressure; closely spaced isobars mean strong wind, widely spaced mean gentle wind. Systems generally move eastward, taking two to three days to cross Australia. **[likely tested]**
- In the southern hemisphere, wind flows anticlockwise out of a high (usually stable, fine weather) and clockwise into a low (unstable, gusty, possibly stormy). **[likely tested]**
- A cold front brings a short burst of violent weather then a shift to stronger west or south west winds; a fast moving band of dark cloud from the south west means seek shelter urgently.
- Land breeze: overnight land cooling gives offshore winds in the morning and typically better sea conditions early. Sea breeze: afternoon onshore winds as the land heats, often strong in summer and feeding late afternoon thunderstorms. Beware overnight anchorages exposed to the west or south west. **[likely tested]**

### Wind and waves

- Wave height depends on wind strength, duration and fetch. Seas roughly double in size over 12 hours of steady wind. Offshore breezes give smaller waves near the windward shore; in a big bay the leeward shore has the smaller waves. **[likely tested]**
- Sea waves are locally wind driven, short and steep. Swell comes from distant weather systems, is long and smooth with a period over 8 seconds, travels fast, and breaks heavily in shallowing water. Sea superimposed on swell can produce unpredictable large waves.
- Swell scales: length short under 100 m, average 100 to 200 m, long over 200 m; height low 0 to 2 m, moderate 2 to 4 m, heavy over 4 m. A growing swell can signal an approaching storm. **[likely tested]**
- Beaufort scale anchors worth memorising: force 0 calm (mirror sea); force 3 gentle breeze 7 to 10 knots; force 4 moderate breeze 10 to 16 knots with waves about 1 to 1.5 m; force 5 fresh breeze 17 to 21 knots; force 6 strong breeze 22 to 27 knots, waves 3 to 4 m (rough); force 8 gale 34 to 40 knots. **[likely tested]**
- On the water: monitor forecasts continuously; watch for darkening lowering cloud, whitecaps and a falling barometer; if whitecaps are visible offshore stay in sheltered water; plan to be upwind of home shelter if wind will rise; put lifejackets on if conditions deteriorate; angle across waves to limit pitching, avoid surfing downwind, and in a sudden squall keep the bow into or slightly off the wind with just enough speed to steer. Rough water can substantially increase fuel consumption. **[likely tested]**

### Tides

- Tides are caused by the gravitational interaction of the sun, moon and earth acting on the oceans, shaped by ocean geometry and weather. Most of the Queensland coast has two high and two low waters a day. **[likely tested]**
- Tidal range varies hugely along the coast: about 6 m around Mackay, about 1 m along the south coast, and under 2 m at Cairns. **[likely tested]**
- Tide times are almost an hour later each day. Spring tides (biggest range) occur at full and new moon; neap tides (smallest range) at the first and third quarter. **[likely tested]**
- Chart datum is the reference level for soundings and tide heights, usually the lowest astronomical tide (LAT). Actual depth equals the charted sounding plus the tide height; a low water below datum is shown with a minus sign. **[likely tested]**
- Queensland Tide Tables (MSQ) give daily predictions for 28 standard ports and conversion data for about 250 secondary places. Secondary place tides are derived by applying a time correction and a height ratio plus correction to the nearest standard port figures. **[likely tested]**
- Weather modifies tides: low pressure raises actual sea level and high pressure lowers it; strong prolonged wind pushes water toward the direction it blows. **[likely tested]**
- Overhead clearances on Queensland charts are measured above highest astronomical tide (HAT), with an extra safety margin built in for power lines. Maximum clearance may not be over the deepest channel, and clearances shrink during king tides and floods. Watch for overhead wires when rigging trailer sailers. **[likely tested]**
- Tidal streams (horizontal water movement with the tide) are distinct from ocean currents. Wind against tide, and tidal races in narrow channels, create hazardous seas; pushing against a foul tide burns extra fuel.

### Coastal bars

- A bar is a shallow bank of sand, mud or gravel near a river mouth or harbour approach; Queensland has many dangerous ones. Crossings should only be attempted with experience, local knowledge, a suitable boat and good conditions.
- Lifejacket law: everyone in an open boat under 4.8 m must wear a lifejacket while crossing a designated coastal bar. **[likely tested]**
- Timing: a bar is generally safest about one hour before high tide, while the flow is still flooding and depths are greatest. The worst state is peak ebb, midway between high and the following low. Never attempt a crossing in heavy swell, strong wind or on a run-out tide; be willing to cancel or delay. **[likely tested]**
- Preparation: check steering, bilge, hatches, drains and watertight integrity, secure loose gear, test engine and controls, draw fuel from a full tank, get a current weather report, tell a volunteer marine rescue group by radio before crossing and report again once clear, and have everyone in lifejackets. **[likely tested]**
- Going out: watch the sets, pick the calmest line and deepest water, and once committed keep going; turning around in front of a breaking wave can be disastrous. Take waves close to head on, do not hit them at speed, aim for the saddles between peaks, accelerate without getting airborne, and note back leads or marks for your return.
- Coming in: a planing boat capable of at least 18 knots should ride the back of a wave, matching the wave's speed, never surfing down the face and never overtaking the wave. Displacement boats must come in very slowly to avoid broaching (being caught side on). In extreme conditions the safest decision may be to stand off in deep water or divert. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s2.9, Weather; s2.10, Tides; s4.8, Bar crossings)

## 11. Trip planning and pre-departure checks

### The voyage plan

- Prepare a plan for every trip, however small. It should record: where you are going; expected time of return; names and phone numbers of everyone on board; fuel carried; safety equipment carried; launch location; a description of the boat; and boat, car and trailer registration numbers. **[likely tested]**
- Leave the plan with a reliable contact and tell them what to do if you are overdue: call triple zero (000) and ask for police. Update the contact if plans change, and report in when you return. **[likely tested]**
- Log on by radio with the local volunteer marine rescue group, especially when going beyond smooth waters, and remember to log off; recent Queensland fatalities highlight the cost of not using this service. **[likely tested]**

### Fuel planning

- Carry enough fuel for the planned trip plus at least one third in reserve; plan to return with at least 30 percent remaining. Calculate consumption for the worst conditions expected, because rough water significantly increases fuel use. **[likely tested]**
- Use fresh fuel (old two stroke premix breaks down and sludges), carry spare fuel in approved airtight containers, and keep tanks topped up and closed to limit condensation.
- Never refill portable fuel tanks in the boat; take them ashore. **[likely tested]**
- Refuelling drill: before - moor securely, shut down engines and electrics, extinguisher handy, hatches closed, bystanders clear, portable tanks ashore; during - keep nozzle contact against static sparks, avoid spills, do not operate radios or phones; after - check for fuel smell in the bilge before starting, and if fuel spilt, pump bilges by hand and ventilate the boat wide open for at least 30 minutes. Leave about 2 percent of tank volume for expansion. **[likely tested]**

### Boat and equipment checks

- The major causes of breakdown at sea are fuel shortage or contamination, mechanical failure, and battery failure. **[likely tested]**
- Check before every trip: no petrol or LPG odours before touching switches (fix any smell first); bilges (excess water means find the fault; oily bilge water must be absorbed, not pumped overboard); engine oil and coolant; battery terminals tight and batteries charged (never use car batteries in boats); engine running properly; fire extinguisher serviced; lights working even for a day trip; electrics; self-drainers clear; ropes and steering cables in good order; anchors rigged and ready; a lifejacket per person, children fitted properly; radio on and tested (ideally by logging on); up to date charts showing ports of refuge; first aid kit complete; tools and spares; gear stowed; sharp knife handy; no overloading; everyone briefed on where safety equipment lives and how the boat and radio work; and the bung in. **[likely tested]**
- If you forget the bung, do not panic: drive the boat fast enough for the water to drain out, then fit the bung. **[likely tested]**
- Routine maintenance: full engine service at least yearly or every 100 hours; replace water pump impellers regularly and confirm water discharge (telltale) on starting; carry spare shear pins, propeller and spanner; clean or replace spark plugs about every 100 hours; change gearbox oil and check the shaft for fishing line (a common cause of leaking seals); keep fuel filters clean; keep electrics corrosion free; replace sacrificial anodes when they are worn to less than 60 percent of original size; grease trailer wheel bearings (the greatest cause of trailer trouble). Flush the engine with fresh water after every salt water use. **[likely tested]**
- LPG is the most dangerous substance on board if mishandled: it is heavier than air and pools in the bilge. Install and service through licensed gas fitters, keep cylinders upright in vented lockers draining overboard, fit flame failure devices or gas detectors, and check joints with soapy water, never a match. **[likely tested]**
- Weather: check the forecast, plan sheltered contingencies. Navigation: know the route and duration, check tides and bar conditions, learn local rules. Clothing: protect from sun, wind and cold, avoid clothing that kills buoyancy, and test that you can swim in your boating clothes.
- Taking off: most injuries happen when people fall overboard while the boat is moving. Nobody rides the bow or side decks or dangles legs; keep weight centred; move off and berth slowly; check for trailing ropes near the propeller.
(Workbook s2.1 to s2.5; Pre-Departure Factsheet; Voyage Plan Template)

### Launching and retrieving

- Prepare away from the ramp (bungs, gear, battery, fuel), remove tie-downs and trailer lights before backing down, attach bow and stern lines, keep the tow vehicle's wheels out of the water, attach a bow line before releasing the winch, and move away from the ramp to load passengers. The speed limit within 30 m of a boat ramp is 6 knots. **[likely tested]**
- Retrieval reverses the process: winch on, engine off before raising, drive clear for cleanup, and pull the bung to drain the bilge.
(Workbook s3.2, Launching and retrieving)

## 12. Manoeuvring and boat handling

### Hulls and propulsion (background for handling questions)

- Displacement hulls plough through the water, carry loads well, need only modest power, and cannot exceed displacement speed without massive extra power. Planing hulls lift and skim on top of the water, are faster and lighter, but are less efficient and less stable at slow speed, so loading is critical. **[likely tested]**
- Other hull forms: semi-displacement (deep V forward, needs big engines to plane, soft offshore ride); catamaran (stable, turns flat); cathedral or tri hull (stable, good turning); inflatables and RIBs (stable, soft wet ride, puncture and UV prone).
- Above about 25 horsepower an outboard should be wheel steered rather than tiller steered, because the torque makes tiller control dangerous. **[likely tested]**
- A kill switch clipped to wrist, jacket or belt cuts the motor if the driver is thrown from the helm; it is an essential safety item. **[likely tested]**
- Petrol inboards are generally avoided: compared with diesel they carry a much higher fire and explosion risk, suffer more from water in the ignition electrics, and produce more toxic exhaust. **[likely tested]**
- Jet drives have no exposed propeller, so are safer around people in the water; most PWC use them.

### Wind, tide and steering effects

- Most boats blow bow-downwind when stationary, and the stern seeks the wind when reversing slowly, because windage is concentrated forward. Leeway (sideways drift) is greatest at slow speed.
- Tidal flow moves the whole boat. Stemming the tide (heading into it) keeps water flowing over the rudder, so an inboard boat keeps steerage even when stationary over the ground. Berth heading into the stream where practicable. **[likely tested]**
- Transverse thrust (paddlewheel effect): a right-handed propeller (turning clockwise in forward, viewed from astern) walks the stern to starboard when going ahead and to port when going astern. It dominates steering in reverse at slow speed. **[likely tested]**
- Practical consequences with a right-handed prop: turning circles are tighter to port; begin a three point turn with the first leg to starboard so reverse swings the stern the helpful way; coming alongside is easier port side to, because engaging reverse walks the stern into the berth; approach a mooring buoy with the buoy slightly on the starboard bow, because the bow swings to starboard when you engage reverse to stop. **[likely tested]**
- Twin screw boats usually have outward-turning counter-rotating props, letting them spin in place by splitting the engines (one ahead, one astern); steer toward the engine that is in forward.
- Pivot point: going ahead a boat pivots well forward of amidships (rear wheel steering, like a forklift); the stern swings wide in turns, the bow cannot turn unless the stern has room, and you cannot simply steer forward away from a dock. In reverse the pivot point moves aft of amidships and the bow follows obediently, so reversing into tight spots is often easier. **[likely tested]**
- Boats respond to helm and throttle after a lag ("advance"); anticipate it in close quarters.
(Workbook s3.1, Factors influencing manoeuvring)

### Berthing, mooring, lines

- A small boat is held alongside with a bow line and a stern line; a bigger boat adds a bow spring and stern spring. On a non-floating berth leave slack for the tide.
- Leaving a berth: get the stern out first, or reverse away (easy with outboards); inboard boats use doubled-up spring lines recovered from on board. Coming alongside: approach at a fine angle, secure a stern line and motor gently ahead to pull alongside, or use a bow spring, or with an inboard use reverse and transverse thrust to walk the stern in.
- Picking up a mooring: read wind and tide from moored boats and the pick-up buoy, approach from downwind or down-tide using the stronger as a brake, come up slowly without overrunning the buoy (mooring lines foul propellers), and secure to a bow cleat. Secure a vessel to a fixed point with a bowline knot (the RMDL practical assessment requires it). **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s3.3 and s3.4; RMDL Standard Unit 5)

## 13. Emergencies

### General emergency response

- Do not panic. Consider weather effects on the boat, get lifejackets on and safety equipment ready, attract attention (wave, flares if warranted), check for fire or explosion risk, block any hull holes with whatever slows the inflow, deploy the V sheet, and call for help on VHF if fitted.
- Stay with the boat. A boat, even partly submerged, is far easier to see than a swimmer and provides support. Distances to shore look shorter over water than they are; swim only if certain land is within reach. Huddle together to cut heat loss. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s5.0 and s5.2)

### Engine failure

- Anchor to stop drift and keep the bow into the sea before attempting repairs; work under a waterproof cover because a wet ignition frustrates restarts. Carry tools, learn basic troubleshooting, and consider an auxiliary motor with its own fuel tank.
(Workbook s5.1)

### Fire

- Fire needs fuel, heat, oxygen and a chemical chain reaction; remove one element to kill it: starve, cool or smother. **[likely tested]**
- Common small boat fire causes: backfiring into fuel vapour, hot exhausts igniting materials, leaking fuel lines spraying hot exhausts, spontaneous combustion of oily rags, static sparks while refuelling, fuel vapours pooling in the bilge, leaking LPG sinking to the bilge, and electrical short circuits or overloads.
- Fighting a fire afloat: raise the alarm, manoeuvre so the wind carries flames outboard (generally run downwind), close hatches and vents to cut oxygen, jettison burning objects if safe, shut fuel and gas lines early, aim the extinguisher into the heart (base) of the fire rather than the flames, and watch for reflash afterwards. Consider isolating the battery. **[likely tested]**
- If abandoning a burning boat, do not exit on the leeward (downwind) side, because the boat or burning fuel on the water can drift onto you. Approach another vessel on fire from windward and stay cautious. **[likely tested]**
- LPG emergency: stop motors, close cylinder valves, turn off appliances, ventilate, and touch no electrical switches until clear. Cool a threatened cylinder with water; if flames threaten to engulf a cylinder, evacuate the boat. **[likely tested]**

### Capsize

- Capsize causes: overloading (reduced freeboard), poor weight distribution, and unexpected waves; aim the bow into waves. Boats without flotation can sink in seconds.
- After capsize: head count, check injuries, stay with the boat, never swim away. Right a small dinghy by gripping the keel, rolling the boat toward you, then transferring grip to the gunwale. Bail out, or paddle ashore if close. **[likely tested]**
- Reboard any boat over the stern, where freeboard is lowest and the boat most stable; never over the side. **[likely tested]**

### Person overboard

- Most boating fatalities involve people falling overboard, even in calm water near shore. Do not jump in after them: the potential drownings immediately double. **[likely tested]**
- Drill: whoever sees the fall alerts the master and never loses sight of the person; turn the bow toward the side they fell (this swings the stern and propeller away) and stop; throw flotation immediately (at night a lit floating torch); fix the position by GPS or shore marks in case outside help is needed. **[likely tested]**
- Recovery may need a rope ladder or rope loop. If someone must enter the water to help, they wear a lifejacket and stay attached to the boat by line. Fallen skiers and swimmers board over the stern with the engine stopped.

### Grounding

- Avoid it: read the beacons, learn the area at low tide, watch for shallow water signs (ripples, colour changes), wear polarised sunglasses, use chart and echo sounder, and test doubtful depth at slow speed.
- A grounded displacement boat or yacht can fall over as the tide leaves; prop hardwood planks under the gunwales, and beware of downflooding as the tide returns if freeboard is low.

### Cold water, survival and first aid

- Immersion in cold water drains body heat up to 25 times faster than air. Hypothermia risks rise with wind chill, wet clothes and age or exhaustion. **[likely tested]**
- Survival positions: HELP (heat escape lessening posture) protects the head, chest sides and groin; huddling in a group can extend survival time by up to 50 percent. Avoid unnecessary swimming: movement pumps heat away. **[likely tested]**
- Hypothermia signs: intense shivering when mild; shivering stops when severe; pale cold skin, slow irregular pulse, slurred speech, confusion, drowsiness.
- Hypothermia treatment: shelter, minimal movement, remove wet clothing, rewarm gradually (space blanket, dry layers, shared body heat), warm the head, neck, chest sides and groin, give warm sweet drinks only if conscious. Never rub or massage limbs, never give alcohol, never use a hot bath or a fire. **[likely tested]**
- More than 80 percent of drowning victims in boating deaths were not wearing a lifejacket, and most deaths involve small boats. **[likely tested]**
- CPR essentials: 30 compressions then 2 breaths; compress the centre of the chest one third of chest depth at about 100 compressions per minute; adults use two hands, children 1 to 8 years the heel of one hand, infants under 1 two fingers; do not tilt an infant's head back; attach a defibrillator as soon as one is available. **[likely tested]**
- DRSABCD order: Danger, Response, Send for help (call 000), Airway, Breathing, CPR (compressions), Defibrillation. **[likely tested]**
- Marine stings: box jellyfish and irukandji stings are potentially fatal; flood the sting with vinegar, watch breathing, get urgent help. Irukandji symptoms can be delayed 5 to 60 minutes after the sting. Blue-ringed octopus and cone shell venom can paralyse breathing within minutes: apply a firm pressure bandage over the whole limb, splint it, and be ready to resuscitate. **[likely tested]**
- Bleeding: direct pressure and elevation; for life threatening limb bleeding an uncovered constrictive bandage at least 5 cm wide; check limb circulation every 30 minutes under a firm dressing.
- Burns: cool with clean fresh water for up to 20 minutes, no ice baths, sea water, lotions or broken blisters. Shock: lay the person down, keep warm, raise the feet about 30 cm, give nothing to eat or drink. Heat exhaustion: shade, cool sponging, fanning, frequent water, call 000. Seasickness: avoid alcohol and rich food, watch the horizon, stay in fresh air, take medication before the trip (watch for drowsiness).
(Workbook s5.3 to s5.8; DRSABCD Factsheet)

## 14. Distress signals and marine radio

### Recognised distress signals

- Continuous sounding of a fog signal
- SOS by any method (three short, three long, three short)
- A mayday call on marine radio
- A red rocket parachute flare or red hand flare at night
- An orange smoke signal by day
- Slowly and repeatedly raising and lowering outstretched arms
- An EPIRB transmission
- A V sheet
- Dye marker by day
- Code flags NC.
Using any of these except to signal genuine distress is illegal and can endanger others. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s6.1, Distress signals)

### Radio equipment

- 27 MHz: cheap and simple, line of sight range, prone to interference; most volunteer marine rescue groups monitor channel 88; larger ships at sea do not listen on 27 MHz; no operator certificate required but recommended. **[likely tested]**
- VHF: the preferred short range radio. Channel 16 is the emergency and initial calling channel, not for chat; channel 67 carries weather in many areas; many rescue stations monitor 16 and 67 around the clock. VHF (and MF/HF) operators must hold an operating certificate, normally the Marine Radio Operators Certificate of Proficiency (MROCP). Station (equipment) licences are no longer needed for 27 MHz or VHF, but are still required for MF/HF. **[likely tested]**
- MF/HF: long range, monitored 24/7 nationally on 4125, 6215 and 8291 kHz; Queensland coast radio stations at Cairns and Gladstone cover coastal waters to at least 200 nm seaward; weather broadcasts on 8176 kHz. **[likely tested]**
- Satellite phones and terminals: expensive but the preferred long range option, especially with HF coast monitoring having an uncertain future.
- More than 40 volunteer rescue stations from Point Danger in the south to St Paul's in the north keep watch. Log on and log off with them for every offshore trip.

### Emergency calls (spoken procedures)

- Mayday: only when the boat is threatened by grave and imminent danger and immediate assistance is required (sinking, on fire). Absolute priority over all traffic; made only on the skipper's authority; transmitted on a distress frequency (VHF 16, 27.88 MHz, or HF 4125, 6215 or 8291 kHz). Format: "mayday" three times; boat name and call sign three times; "mayday"; name and call sign again; position; nature of distress and help needed; other details (people on board, description, intentions). **[likely tested]**
- Pan pan: the urgency call, for a very urgent message about the safety of the boat or a person when distress is not justified (disabled and drifting toward a lee shore, seriously ill crew member). Same frequencies. Format: "pan pan" three times; "hello all stations" three times; boat name and call sign three times; position; assistance needed. **[likely tested]**
- Securite (pronounced say-cure-e-tay): the safety call for important navigational or weather warnings (a large floating object, storm warnings). Usually made by coast stations. Initial call may go out on a distress frequency but the message body moves to a working frequency. **[likely tested]**
- Routine calls: name the station called (up to three times), "this is" plus your boat name (up to three times), await reply, pass the message, end with "over". Always listen before transmitting.

### Mobile phones

- Phones are backup only, never a substitute for marine radio: no distress priority in the cellular network; coverage, battery and water damage risks; point to point only, so nearby boats cannot hear a call for help; rescue direction finders cannot trace a phone; and few rescue vessels carry phones, so messages get relayed and distorted. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s6.2 and s6.3)

## 15. Waterskiing and towing rules

- Waterskiing means towing a person on skis, bare feet, inflatable toys, boards or a parasail. The owner or master is responsible for confirming the waterway is safe: depth, turning width, hazards.
- Keep at least 30 m from people in the water, anchored or moored ships, boat ramps, jetties and pontoons while towing; keep as far as possible from banks and shorelines. **[likely tested]**
- In rivers and creeks, ski traffic travels anticlockwise unless local custom or signage says otherwise. **[likely tested]**
- The towed person must wear a level 50, level 50 special purpose, or level 100 lifejacket, or a wetsuit with built-in buoyancy (approved as level 50 special purpose in smooth waters; at least level 50 in partially smooth waters). **[likely tested]**
- A capable observer aged 13 years or older must be on board watching the skier at all times; the operator holds a marine licence and keeps a proper lookout ahead, not on the skier. The observer relays the skier's signals and reports falls immediately. **[likely tested]**
- The ski tip should show above the water before the boat gets under way. A fallen skier who is unhurt clasps hands overhead until the boat sees them. Recover skiers with the engine stopped at a safe distance; skiers leave their skis in the water and board over the stern. **[likely tested]**
- Agreed hand signals (faster, slower, turn, stop, cut motor, back to dock, OK) should be arranged before the tow.
- PWC towing: allowed using a cruising PWC that seats two or more, so the observer can be carried; all normal waterskiing rules apply. **[likely tested]**
(Workbook s4.10, Waterskiing rules; s7.5, PWC waterskiing)

## 16. PWC-specific knowledge

### How a PWC works

- A PWC is a small, high powered, jet driven inboard boat capable of high speed and radical manoeuvres. An engine drives a pump; water is drawn through an intake grate, pressurised by an impeller and expelled through a steerable nozzle.
- The jet stream can throw water up to 3 m behind the craft and can injure swimmers; keep the intake grate clear of people, hair and clothing. **[likely tested]**
- A PWC needs at least about 600 mm of water depth to avoid sucking up sand and damaging the impeller. **[likely tested]**
- The single most critical PWC handling fact: steering requires power. With the throttle off or the engine stopped, most PWC lose all steering and continue straight ahead no matter where the bars point. To avoid an obstacle, keep the power on and turn; do not chop the throttle. **[likely tested]**
- PWC have no neutral: as soon as the engine starts the craft moves forward. **[likely tested]**
- From full speed a PWC can travel up to 80 m after the throttle is released, because boats have no brakes. **[likely tested]**
- The reverse lever swings a bucket over the jet nozzle; it must only be applied at slow idle and never used as a brake, or the craft can be damaged.
- Speed regimes: trolling (idle, bow down, no wake), sub-planing (bow up, stern deep, big wake), planing (level and skimming, minimal wake). At speed, turn like a motorcycle: slight speed reduction, lean, then throttle through the turn; too little throttle and it will not turn, too much and it spins out.
- A typical PWC fuel tank holds around 60 litres.
- The most common cause of PWC breakdown is a blocked impeller. Cavitation (engine revs rise while thrust falls) signals a blockage; stop the engine and remove the lanyard before clearing the intake. **[likely tested]**

### Safety systems

- PWC have either a kill switch lanyard or a self-circling feature. The lanyard attaches the operator to the ignition safety switch (via lifejacket or wrist); if the operator falls off, the engine stops. Test it before every trip.
- A self-circling PWC idles in a circle after the rider falls off and returns toward the fall-off point; never swim after it, wait for it to come back. **[likely tested]**
- Capsize righting: most PWC must be rolled back upright in one specific direction (check the hull decal or manual, typically rolled left to right); rolling the wrong way can damage the engine. Stop the engine (remove the lanyard) first, keep hands out of the intake grille, and beware of waves. Reboard over the back deck, not the side, and head in to check for water damage. **[likely tested]**
- Ride in pairs where possible, especially offshore or wave jumping; carry a hand-held VHF or phone since most PWC cannot mount a full marine radio. Consider a tow rope, spare bungs, spare lanyard, helmet, gloves and booties.
- Passengers hold the lifejacket straps of the person in front, never the rear seat handle (arm and back injuries in a capsize or spin-out). Brief passengers, and check the manufacturer's age and size limits, which can affect insurance. **[likely tested]**

### PWC distance and speed laws

A PWC must slow to 6 knots (about 11 km/h) or keep the stated distance:

- 60 m from people in the water **[likely tested]**
- 60 m from anchored or moored boats, boat ramps, jetties and pontoons **[likely tested]**
- 60 m from the shore **[likely tested]**
- 60 m from the boundary of a bathing reserve. **[likely tested]**

Note the contrast: the general boating distance rule is 6 knots within 30 m; for PWC the distances double to 60 m. **[likely tested]**

Exceptions to 6 knots within 60 m of the shore: a waterway narrower than 120 m may be transited as close as practicable to a straight line near the centre or the marked channel; and PWC engaged in waterskiing or towing. **[likely tested]**

- A PWC travelling faster than 10 knots must keep at least 30 m from other moving vessels (unless in an approved aquatic event). **[likely tested]**
- Judging distance: 60 m is 10 m longer than an Olympic swimming pool.
- Maintain 6 knots or less until 60 m from shore when launching, and the same when beaching; slow further near rocks or ramps. **[likely tested]**

### Freestyling and surf

- Freestyling is erratic, non-directional, unpredictable driving: wake jumping, donuts, figure eights, 360s and similar high speed manoeuvres. **[likely tested]**
- In coastal waters, freestyling and wave jumping must occur more than 200 m from shore where dwellings within 100 m of the shoreline are visible and nearby. Coastal waters here exclude dams and non-tidal inland waters. **[likely tested]**
- Wave jumpers must stay at least 60 m from bathing reserves and people in the water. Check for swimmers and board riders hidden behind wave crests, know the landing zone, and beware of sandbars. Riders separated from the craft mid-jump are a common injury cause.
- Multiple PWC freestyling in one area should agree on a common direction, for example anticlockwise.
- Meet waves head on going out; ride behind (not on the face of) waves coming in; broaching on the lip of a wave can wreck craft and rider.
- Marine zones under the 2016 Regulation can exclude PWC or specific activities from an area. PWC have been prohibited on Tallebudgera Creek since 1997, and the Noosa River Marine Zone restricts PWC, waterskiing, freestyling, hovercraft and airboats. Waterways management plans on the Gold and Sunshine Coasts show where freestyling is banned. **[likely tested]**
- Courtesy and noise: PWC attract more noise and nuisance complaints than any other craft. Avoid manoeuvres that lift the exhaust out of the water, keep away from houses, parks, busy beaches, ramps and roosting waterbirds. The 85 dB(A) at 30 m engine noise cap includes PWC.
(Workbook s7.0, Personal watercraft; PWCL Standard)

### PWC pre-departure and assessment points

- Before launching: check hull for cracks and leaks, secure bungs, test steering and throttle, check battery, fuel and oil, check spark plugs and electrics, confirm the intake grate is clear, and lock down compartments and seats. Fuel on land where possible.
- The PWCL practical assessment expects candidates to judge distances of 30 m and 60 m and speeds of 6 knots and 10 knots, perform a figure eight, come alongside floating and fixed objects, and demonstrate righting knowledge. PWC riders must understand why the extra speed of a PWC shrinks the time available to assess collision risk. **[likely tested]**
(PWCL Standard Units 1 to 3; Workbook s7.3)

## 17. Good seamanship for small and medium boats

### Loading and trim

- Trim is the fore and aft balance of the boat. Stow heavy items low and central, distribute weight evenly to avoid list, and keep bilges dry, decks clear and drains free.
- Freeboard is the safety margin between the water and the gunwale; overloading eats it. Respect the ABP capacity and cut it by a third outside smooth waters. **[likely tested]**
- Keep passengers inside the boat, seated, centred, and off the bow and side decks whenever the boat is moving.

### Wash and courtesy

- Watch your wake: you are responsible for damage or danger your wash causes to other boats, people in the water, and the shore, regardless of posted speed limits. Slow down through mooring areas and near small craft, and watch for people in the water and trailing lines. **[likely tested]**
- There is no substitute for care, courtesy and common sense toward other water users and people on shore. Courtesy at ramps: prepare away from the ramp, launch efficiently, move clear to load.

### Lines and knots

- Use a bowline to secure the boat to fixed points; it is the knot required in the RMDL practical assessment. **[likely tested]**
- Berthing lines: bow line, stern line, plus bow and stern springs on larger boats. Keep all ropes in good condition and stowed ready for use, and keep a sharp knife handy.
- Nylon rope: strong, stretchy, abrasion resistant, best for anchoring. Silver rope: cheaper, weaker. Polypropylene floats and must not be used for anchor lines. **[likely tested]**

### Fuel management and engine sense

- Plan fuel as trip fuel plus one third reserve, budgeted at worst-case consumption; arrive home with 30 percent in the tank. Rough water and headwinds against tide raise consumption. **[likely tested]**
- Keep the fuel system inspected and leak free; your nose is the most important fuel system diagnostic tool: if you smell fuel, find the problem before starting anything. **[likely tested]**
- Outboards like regular use; raise the leg when moored (weed and coral choke the intake), flush after salt water use, and follow the 100 hour service rhythm.

### Navigation basics

- One knot equals one nautical mile per hour; a nautical mile is one minute of latitude, about 1852 m. A boat making 1 knot covers 1 nm in one hour, which lets you estimate distance run from speed and time with just a chart, compass and watch. **[likely tested]**
- A magnetic compass points to magnetic north, which differs from true north by the magnetic variation shown on the chart's compass rose; variation changes over time and must be applied. In Queensland it ranges from about 5 degrees east in Torres Strait to about 12 degrees east at Point Danger. **[likely tested]**
- Rain can cut visibility to under 500 m; without a compass it is easy to become completely lost. Carry chart and compass even if you have GPS.
- GPS limitations: battery dependence, datum mismatches with charts (boats have grounded from trusting GPS alone), zoom abuse beyond chart accuracy, and initialisation time. Verify GPS positions against the chart and compass, keep electronic charts updated, check the track to a waypoint for hazards, and keep a proper lookout. Dim screens at night to preserve night vision. **[likely tested]**
- Night navigation: depth perception and distance judgement degrade, unlit buoys are hard to spot, city lights confuse; slow down, use a spotlight sparingly and never into another skipper's eyes, keep passengers seated, and have everyone in lifejackets. **[likely tested]**
- The Beacon to Beacon Directory maps waterways from Tweed Heads to Yeppoon, including local rules.
(Workbook s1.2 to s1.6; s4.6, Navigating)
